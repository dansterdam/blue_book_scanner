UNCLASSIFIED

AF FORM 112-PART II
APPROVED 1 JUNE 1948

AIR INTELLIGENCE INFORMATION REPORT

D/Intelligence, Hq 2AF                     IR-36-53             PAGE    2    OF    3    PAGES

The following information on an unidentified flying object was submitted to this office by Major Jack D Swickard, 1142CM:

1. The object appeared to be a reddish ball of fire, approximately three times the size of a large star. The object moved very fast in a straight line. The speed of the object was described as considerably faster than a jet aircraft and was judged by several B-47's in the traffic pattern in the general vicinity of the observation.

2. The sighting occurred shortly after 2100 hours on 14 August 1953. The length of the sighting was described as only a matter of seconds.

3. This was a visual sighting.

4. At the time of the sighting, Major Swickard and a base camp warden, S Sgt Otto Rector, 1146077, were both at the north end of Flags Lake situated in the north central part of the Base/Flight area. Swickard was watching a northerly direction and moved toward the southeast, moving almost parallel to the earth's surface and rising slightly before disappearing from view.

5. Major Swickard is an Aircraft Observer and believed very reliable. He has studied Astronomy and very definitely stated that this unidentified object was not a "shooting star". S Sgt Rector equally only a slim slice of the object when Major Swickard shouted "look" to him. However, S Sgt Rector stated that he has observed similar objects on three other occasions, but did not report them because of fear of ridicule.

6. The weather at the time was described by Major Swickard as a very clear, cloudless night with the sky full of stars. However, there was no cloud at the time of the sighting.

7. At 2100 hours, on the date of the observation, the Barksdale Weather Station released a weather balloon.

8. No fragments, photographs, etc., were obtained.

9. There was no known interception attempted.

10. At the time of this sighting, there were several low aircraft shooting, later to be a search visual, the B-47's were in the traffic pattern and from the observers. Immediately after the observation, a B-47 came overhead and formed the basis for Major Swickard's judgment of the speed of the unidentified object.

Another Observation: A local newspaper stated that several people in nearby Shreveport at 9:30 hours on the same date saw an unidentified flying object. The newspaper account was vague and could not be substantiated.
