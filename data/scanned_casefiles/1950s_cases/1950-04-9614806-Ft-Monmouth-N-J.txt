UNCLASSIFIED UNCONVENTIONAL AIRCRAFT

17 April 1950

MEMORANDUM TO THE OFFICER IN CHARGE

1. At approximately 1830 hours on 14 April 1950 Investigator, Military Intelligence, Fort Monmouth, N.J., interviewed M/Sgt. at his residence, Main St., Fort Monmouth, mid-Middletown Township, N. J., regarding his observing unconventional aircraft in flight.

He stated that at approximately 1430 hours on 14 April 1950, while motoring in a northeasterly direction on Wilson Ave. approximately one quarter mile south of State Highway #36 and approaching Fort Monmouth, he observed four (4) objects in the sky. He immediately stopped his vehicle and dismounted for a better view. The four objects were approaching from the vicinity of Keyport, N. J., and were flying south in the direction of Sea Bright, N. J.

The objects were described as follows: Each object appeared to be rectangular in shape, approximately 3' wide to 3' 4' long, 11' thick, amber colored, entirely flat without concave or convex bulges, without wings, tails or wheels. The objects were grouped together in a fairly close disorganized formation and appeared to be flying at an altitude of approximately 2,000 feet with an air speed of approximately 75 - 100 miles per hour. The position of each object in the formation was constantly changing in a rapid but non-systematic manner, without a definite pattern. The objects would either accelerate, decelerate, move right or move left very rapidly.

The entire formation constantly rose and fell in a smooth coordinated movement although each object was constantly changing its position in the formation. The estimated loss and gain of altitude with each up and down movement was approximately 1,000 feet. The air-speed of the group was constant and appeared not to gain speed when climbing. Cable string or towing apparatus was not visible in the sky, nor were there conventional aircraft present in the vicinity.

Visibility was fair with cloud-banks at an altitude of approximately 3,000 feet. Three or four minutes lapsed from the time the objects were first observed until they were lost from sight.
