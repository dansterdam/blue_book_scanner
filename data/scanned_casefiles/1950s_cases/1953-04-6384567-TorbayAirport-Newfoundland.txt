1. At 1214012, Master Sergeant Crockett sighted an unidentified target on the GCA scope at Torbay Airport, Newfoundland. At approximately 1700 hrs, 14 Mar 53, a/c was on a northwesterly course at Torbay Airport, Newfoundland. Master Sergeant Crockett, Class 1 GCA operator, was on duty at the GCA trailer in order to monitor the GCA trailers in order to observe visible traffic. The aircraft was flying just outside the GCA traffic pattern in one mile range. Approximately 270 degrees from the airport, Sergeant Crockett returned to the scope, and noticed the unidentified aircraft in a magnetic course of 90 degrees. The aircraft was making a wide arc to the left on a magnetic course of 270 degrees until it was 10 miles west of the airport, then turned and proceeded on a magnetic course of 90 degrees.

2. The Torbay tower operator did not see this aircraft on its scope after being notified of the incident. Sergeant Crockett was the only person on duty in the GCA Operations, notified of the incident on the GCA scope. Major Toohy, who contacted the Intelligence Officer at Pepperrell Air Force Base at 1630.

3. After transmission of the initial message of the sighting, the Director of Intel, MIAC, was contacted by telephone and was given the details of the sighting by Sergeant Crockett. The results were negative. Capt Morehouse, Air Control, was contacted. He stated that B-17 and B-29 aircraft were flying in the area, and he doubted that an aircraft was sighted, stating possibly position the aircraft at Torbay at the sighting time. That no B-17 or B-29 was known to be in the area taken for a B-29 as it is the cargo version of the Super Fortress. Upon contacting Pepperrell Base Operations, USAF, who checked the log of Torbay Airport with positive position reports, all leads were exhausted.

COMMENTS of the Preparing Officer:

1. Weather sequence encompassing the incident sighting was 10 miles visibility, 270 feet scattered, 1200 feet broken, 2000 feet overcast, wind 7 knots.

2. Master Sergeant Crockett has 12 years military service. He is a former B-17 pilot with 1200 flying hours of which 1000 were in B-17. He had previously been stationed at Hill AFB, Utah, Goose Bay, Newfoundland, Florida where B-50s and B-29s are assigned, thereby conclusively establishing himself with B-50 and B-29 aircraft.

3. The original purpose of Sergeant Crockett's reporting this incident was to obtain a violation of the unconventional approach over the airport. Although initially it was observed as a significant intelligence, it was phased into a vital intelligence incident.

4. From the foregoing available information, there appears to be only three possible explanations of this incident.

a. That subject aircraft was in fact the G-97 aircraft to be used by the pilot, that while on a flight, they were somehow unable to get out of the Torbay weather pattern, and had to give a nearby position.

b. That subject aircraft was a U. S. aircraft on a classified mission, the flight plan of which has not been made available to this headquarters.

c. That subject aircraft was a Soviet TU-4 aircraft whose radio was unidentified by aerial refueling.

5. Intelligence presently available on the Soviet TU-4, however, is based on guessing.

The Soviets are in possession of a few U. S. B-29's that were intercepted during World War II.

a. That recent photographs of the TU-4 show it to be no exact replica of the B-29, insofar as external appearance are concerned.

b. From the known characteristics of the B-29, the characteristics of the TU-4 have been arrived at by deduction.

c. The Soviets have been very careful to conduct TU-4 flight operations within the Soviet Union. Only recently have TU-4's been seen in certain satellite countries, and then only to a very limited degree.

6. The Director of Intelligence, USAF, believes it to be very unlikely that the Soviets would risk sending an aircraft of this type into an area where there was possibly a U. S. facility at the time. Accordingly, he would suggest that it is to detailed examination by Western technical experts with the result that additional information on Soviet aerial activity might be forthcoming.

Present, is conjecture, to include information on Soviet aerial activity being known. I view, or the foregoing, it is the considered opinion of the Director of Intelligence, USAF, that although surface analysis tends to lean toward the possibility, there is reason. The Director of Intelligence, USAF, therefore recommends that a detailed study be made.
