Unidentified Flying Object Report

A bright, flare-like explosion was sighted by the crew of an Air Force C-54 approximately two hour's flying time west of Wake Island. During the tour within which the sighting occurred, one visual and two radar contacts were made. Tracing action has uncovered no routine sea or air traffic in the area at the time of sighting.

Description of Object:
1. Shape: Circular (Fire-ball)
2. Perspective: Unclear
3. Color: Greenish-blue (TYPICAL FIREBALL CHARACTERISTICS)
4. One
5. N/A
6. Bright, instantaneous flash.
7. None observed.
8. None observed.
9. The "fireball" was observed directly only by the flight engineer; however the light of the flash startled both pilots. The engineer was looking through a window at the rear starboard side of the cabin. Two pilots were both at their seats in the cockpit, separated from the cabin by a closed and lined crew compartment.

Course of Flight: The flash was instantaneous, and therefore showed no apparent motion.

Observation:
1. Air-visual, direct by one observer and indirect by two.
2. None.
3. C-54D, 42-72564, 7,700 lbs., 126° TN, 120 kts. (found speed), 153 kts IAS, 165 kts TAS. (Yokota AB, Japan)

Time and Date:
1. 1300LMZ February 1953, 0944+12 = 2114
2. Night.

Observers: Aircraft position was 21°N 143°E. Observers were located as follows:
1. Flight engineer at "station B" window (side in turbulence next to starboard side of cabin)
2. Aircraft commander was in the cockpit, left seat, looking directly at the cockpit.
3. Co-pilot was in the cockpit, right seat, scanning the left side of the instrument panel.
4. Navigator and radio operator were in the limited crew compartment, with cockpit curtain drawn and cabin door closed, and thus were not knowledgeable of the sighting until after it occurred.

3. Narrative Summary: The mission commander and pilot of aircraft 52-2630 (C-54) and 52-2632 (C-54) were flying at a range of 20 miles and bearing of approximately 325 degrees relative to the aircraft. Finally, under the 10th GTG, the mission commander noticed a blip at a hospital or approximately 225 and hardly within the five-mile range. Airborne radar altitudes were operatively made of objects at a lower altitude than the reporting aircraft.

4. First Radar Analysis: Sightings could have been of the same physical object; the first radar contact might have been lost due to the 50-700 "shadow" on the APAR-42 scope resolution. The mission commander was assuming that the two radar blips represented the same physical object - an aircraft. For example - the object would have had no bearing good a ground speed of 175 knots. If this assumption were correct, the object (or aircraft) would have been flying a converging course, at a lower altitude, bearing into the radar "shadow" of the reporting aircraft between 0005 and 0010, and appearing again when the reporting aircraft reversed course to 224 degrees. The pilot's memory of radar "shadow" under earlier part of the mission indicates that one other aircraft was outbound behind the reporting aircraft. The pilot's report also indicates that another aircraft (C-542 42-7523) limped at Wake for 30 minutes under the reporting aircraft.

5. Detailed Analysis: Such flashes, such aircraft, such ground, are available in the PACAF inventory. The description of the flash would also apply to a photo-flash cartridge. However, photo cartridges are fired down, not upward, and there is no other information to suggest an aircraft at a different altitude. At the time of the sighting, the pilot was in the area of sighting. As a result of this preliminary investigation, it is determined that another aircraft, at a lower altitude (C-542 42-7523), may be the cause of the sighting described.
