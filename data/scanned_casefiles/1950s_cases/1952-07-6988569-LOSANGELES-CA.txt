Second Lieutenant John L. Speigel, Intelligence Officer

Dear Lieutenant Speigel:

I understand from our local paper that the Army Air Force is requesting all observations of "unidentified flying objects" to be reported to them.

On the following pages you will find a detailed description of such a sighting viewed by myself and family on the 26th of July at 11:05 P.M.

I personally feel that many reported accounts are highly erroneous and can easily be explained by the activity of local aircraft, searchlights, etc. It may well be that such is the case in this instance and I have therefore signed affidavit attached hereto. If you inquire further into this matter, I can produce such possibilities or move our observations to be of a character easily explained.

For your further information it is my opinion that many observations or sightings have been made by people not specifically qualified to report even the conditions surrounding such observations. I am therefore listing my background so that you may judge the probable accuracy of my report.

My own conclusions are that the objects sighted were definitely not reflections of ground objects, but a temperature inversion.

They were furthermore not reflections on the glass windows of the car in which I was travelling but stood out and was not out of same.

They were too bright for identification lights on planes.

Should further information be desired I can be reached at York or Millersville.

I would greatly appreciate a reply as to your findings in the matter.

Very truly yours,

REPORT OF SIGHTING

Date - July 28th, 1952
Time - First seen about 11:01 P. M. Last seen at 11:09 P. M. when I moved car to spot just North of Newberrytown and they were no longer visible.
Place of Sighting - About 2 1/2 miles North of Newberrytown on U.S. Highway #11 (1 mile from end of present four lane road). Drove one mile in about one minute while watching lights and stopped car. Objects did not seem to be moving in sky. (Note this can be movement proviso: triangulation base for estimated distance noted later). Next observation made after stopping car and getting out of same about one mile North of Newberrytown.

Estimated Line of Flight - Probably first sighted over Susquehanna River in vicinity of Goldsboro and last saw them in vicinity of Highspire.

Estimated Altitude - Between one and two thousand.

Estimated Speed - Usually they gave the appearance of traveling at about 80 or 90 M.P.H.

Estimated Distance - Three to Four Miles.

Description of objects - They consisted of two (2), very constant intensity, sources of light. I can estimate intensity of approximately eight to ten, sometimes the magnitude of brightness of the North Star on a clear night. The color was in the pink part of the spectrum, perhaps due to haze. There were two or these lights spaced at an estimated slightly mile with the one to the rear slightly higher in elevation than the lead object. The lead object descended somewhat when passing the sky glow from Olmstead Field.

No smoke trail or light rays were otherwise visible nor the lights. Their speed was relatively constant. The objects seemed to be affected by thermal up-drafts and down-drafts. My oldest son made this observation which corrorated my own without any prompting or previous mention of the fact on my part.

There were no red port lights visible which should have been the case if they were planes.

The intensity of these two lights did not change which they would definitely have done had they been tail lights on two planes traveling thru the area they seemed to describe.

Atmospheric Conditions - Slight haze with ceiling estimated at 7,000. One very bright star visible. Temperature to west of Olmstead Field more brighter. Several miles of these objects when lightning strokes illuminated sky. At no time was sky completely dark. During this time, I could see no physical outline of any object however in spite of my extensive use of this sort of thing in close photography.
