ENCLOSURE #1

STATEMENT BY CAPTAIN DANIEL

I was the Aerial Weather Reconnaissance Officer on the night of 1 November 1956 in a T-39 aircraft going from Springfield, Illinois to Eglin Air Force Base on a true course of 140°. After reaching an altitude of 35,000 feet, a steady orange light with a blue tinge was sighted near Mount Vernon at about 230° at about a 5° elevation angle and 220° from the aircraft. I was unable to judge the distance, speed, or size of the object. There was no tail, or exhaust, or ashcan. I did not appear to be a jet exhaust as the light intensity was constant when viewed from a changing direction. I do not believe it was a meteor as there was not any tail, nor evident forward motion. I was able to view the object from the aircraft for about two minutes, increasing nearly 5° in elevation and to about 320° from the aircraft in position. As the aircraft was heading in opposite direction from the object, I was unable to view it any longer. The night was clear, with an estimated ceiling and unrestricted visibility of at least 60 miles. The cloud formation in the area was thin altostratus and the East and Southeast horizon was clear in the vicinity of the object. I am unable to give a logical explanation for this reported sighting.

The above statement was given to Captain William H. Lyons by Capt Daniel.
